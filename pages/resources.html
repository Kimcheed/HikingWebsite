Resources for Hiking

Hiking presents a lovely opportunity for anyone to enjoy the beauty of nature and all of the its natural wonders. Whether you are an avid trekker or first time hiker, it is best to be prepared.

Important Information!

Types of hiking include trekking, mountaineering, backpacking, dayhiking and a basic 1-2 mile hike.

  • Trekking and backpacking are forms of hiking that involve more than several miles or at least an over-night camp or more.
  • Mountainerring involves hiking or to summit up a mountain which may take a day or more, but only for the true risk-takers.
  • Day-hiking essentially means the amount of hiking involved in a day and of course the basic 1-2 mile hike which is a strong suggestion for entry-level hikers. The basic 1-2 mile hike usually involve trails that are popular and present more frequent encounters with other hikers and last no longer than 2 hours.

For the purpose of the University Hiking Club, resources for basic day-hiking will be the main focus of this page. Basic day-hiking correspond with the trails from the Locations page.

Types of Trails

Trail types relevent to our hiking experience in the club are the loop, out-and-back, and the point to point.

    Rainbow Mountain Loop

  • Loop: The trail starts and ends at the same trail head.
    • Example: Rainbow Mountain Loop
    • Length: 1.5 Miles

    Kings Chair Overlook

  • Out-and-back: trails start at a trailhead, follow another route, but end back at the beginning trailhead.
    • Example: Oak Mountain State Park's Kings Chair Overlook
    • Length: 3.3 Miles

    Chewacla Creek Paddle Route

  • Point-to-point: a trail starts at one location and end at another location. (Point a to Point b)
    • Chewacla Creek Paddle Route
    • Length: 9.1 Miles

Support

Trekking Poles

Trekking poles are strongly advised for longer hikes, however; trekking Poles or walking sticks come in handy more than one could imagine. For instance:

  • Additional support during inclinations, declinations, wet, or rough terrain
  • Provides protection from wildlife such as snakes or other forms
  • Used to check obstruction such as bushes or possibly move obstructions

Need to Know for Entry Level Hikers

  • Do not go alone. Grab a friend to accompany you on your first hike or better yet, join a club.

  • Let family and other friends know where you are going before you set out on your first hike.

  • It is best for entry level hikers to hike short distances at first (1-2 miles). It is important to know your level of physical activity.

  • Research the trail or terrain before your first hike and make sure you have reliable navigation or at at least a physical map of the trail.

  • Check the weather forecast a week prior to your hike. Keep in mind if it rained a day or two before the intended hike then expect the terrain to be quite muddy or slippery.

  • Make sure you bring proper equipment according to the hike.
